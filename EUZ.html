Background

Umpires and strikezones

Consider a typical at-bat during a game of Major League Baseball (MLB):

  • the pitcher throws (pitches) the baseball towards the hitter
  • the hitter may attempt to swing at the ball
  • if the hitter chooses not to swing, the umpire calls the pitch either
    • a strike if the ball falls within the strikezone
    • a ball if the ball falls outside the strikezone
  • three strikes result in the hitter getting called out, while four balls result in the hitter getting to proceed to first base

The umpires call is thus extremely important for how a game proceeds. Furthermore, the umpires call is not reviewable. It is common for players and fans to direct anger at umpires for poor calls, particularly if they occur during a high-stakes moment.

Thanks to a plethora of available data, fans have taken it upon themselves to track ump performance. Ump Scorecards in particular tweets a visual summary of each umps performance after every game. The objective of this tutorial is to show how to produce an Estimated Umpire Zone (EUZ) in R. The EUZ shows how the umps called balls and strikes differ from the official strike zone, and allows one to measure an umps internal consistency (that is, does an ump follow their own strike zone?).

To compare to the official strikezone, we need to know its dimensions:

The official strike zone is the area over home plate from the midpoint between a batters shoulders and the top of the uniform pants when the batter is in his stance and prepared to swing at a pitched ball and a point just below the kneecap.

mlb.com

In other words, the strike zone depends on the physiology of each player! Figure 1 shows an approximate average strike zone based on Hunter (2018).

Classification

Computing the EUZ is a classification task. Specifically, we want to model the probability a pitch is called a strike as a function of the \(x\) (horizontal) and \(z\) (vertical) coordinates of the point where the baseball crossed home plate. Denote this by \(\Pr(\text{strike}\,\, |\,\, x,z)\). Then the EUZ is the set of \((x,z)\) coordinates defined by \(\Pr(\text{strike}\,\, |\,\, x,z) > 0.5\), that is, it is the area where a pitch is more likely to be called a strike than a ball.

We take two approaches to estimating this probability. First, we use k nearest neighbours (KNN), which is a non-parametric approach to directly estimating \(\Pr(\text{strike}\,\, |\,\, x,z)\). Second, we use several models based on Bayes theorem:

\[ \Pr(\text{strike}\,\, |\,\, x,z) = \frac{f(x,z\,\,|\,\,\text{strike})\Pr(\text{strike})}{f(x,z\,\,|\,\,\text{strike})\Pr(\text{strike}) + f(x,z\,\,|\,\,\text{ball})\Pr(\text{ball})} \]

By using Bayes theorem, we decompose the problem into two parts:

  • Estimate \(\Pr(\text{ball})\) and \(\Pr(\text{strike})\) based on the sample proportions of balls and strikes.
  • Estimate the joint density functions \(f(x,z\,\,|\,\,\text{strike})\) and \(f(x,z\,\,|\,\,\text{ball})\).

We summarise the Bayes-theorem-based methods below, and the assumptions they make about the joint densities:

  • Quadratic discriminant analysis (QDA): \(X,Z\,\,|\,\,\text{call} \sim \text{N}(\vec{\mu}_\text{call},\Sigma_\text{call})\)
  • Naive Bayes (NB) with Kernel Density Estimation (KDE):
    • Assume independence: \(f(x,z\,\,|\,\,\text{call}) = f_x(x\,\,|\,\,\text{call})f_z(z\,\,|\,\,\text{call})\)
    • \(f_x,f_z\) estimated non-parametrically using KDE
  • Multivariate KDE (MKDE): \(f(x,z\,\,|\,\,\text{call})\) estimated non-parametrically using MKDE

We rely on the baseballr package to collect Statcast pitching data from MLB. For the sake of this tutorial we look at the 2022 home opener that the Toronto Blue Jays (TOR) played against the Texas Rangers (TEX) on April 8th. Results

Quadratic Discriminant Analysis

To plot the EUZ, well need to predict probability of a strike at many points in the x-z plane and find the curve where the probability is 0.5. We store these points in the matrix d, which we will use for QDA and all other methods.

x = seq(-3,3,0.02)
z = seq(0,6,0.02)
d = expand.grid(x,z)
names(d) = c("plate_x","plate_z")

Now we estimate QDA model and predict on d:

model.qda = qda(description ~ plate_x + plate_z, data = TORgame)
d$ypred = predict(model.qda, d)$posterior[,1]

Now we plot the contour over top of our Figure 2 plot:

# theme
ggthemr("flat")

# plot
gg.QDA = Figure2 +
  stat_contour(aes(x=plate_x,y=plate_z,z=ypred), data=d, breaks=c(0.5),
               geom="polygon",fill=adjustcolor("firebrick3",alpha.f=0.2))

# figure notes
annotate_figure(gg.QDA,
  bottom=text_grob(
    "Note: The black rectangle represents the approximate rule book strike zone.\n Red shaded area represents the Estimated Ump Zone using each methodology.\n Inside the area, the model predicts a strike. Outside, the model predicts a ball.\nSource: Statcast Pitch Data (Toronto vs. Texas, April 2 2022).",
    hjust=0,x=unit(0,"npc")))

0.0.1 Naive Bayes with Kernel Density Estimation

Unlike with QDA, we will need to implement Bayes rule ourselves. The easy part is the prior probabilities:

p_strike = sum(TORgame$description=="called_strike")/nrow(TORgame)
p_ball   = sum(TORgame$description=="ball")/nrow(TORgame)

Next we need the densities. Because it is Naive Bayes, we naively assume independence between x and z. To evaluate the densities at the coordinates in our d matrix, we use the kde function from the ks package.

# density | strike
strikedata = TORgame %>%
  filter(description=="called_strike") %>%
  select(c(plate_x,plate_z))
dens_strike = 
  kde(x=as.matrix(strikedata["plate_x"]),
      h=hpi(as.matrix(strikedata["plate_x"])),
      density=TRUE, eval.points=d[,1])$estimate *
  kde(x=as.matrix(strikedata["plate_z"]),
      h=hpi(as.matrix(strikedata["plate_z"])),
      density=TRUE, eval.points=d[,2])$estimate

# density | ball
balldata = TORgame %>%
  filter(description=="ball") %>%
  select(c(plate_x,plate_z))
dens_ball = 
  kde(x=as.matrix(balldata["plate_x"]),
      h=hpi(as.matrix(balldata["plate_x"])),
      density=TRUE, eval.points=d[,1])$estimate *
  kde(x=as.matrix(balldata["plate_z"]),
      h=hpi(as.matrix(balldata["plate_z"])),
      density=TRUE, eval.points=d[,2])$estimate

Finally, we apply Bayes rule:

d$ypred = (dens_strike*p_strike) / (dens_ball*p_ball)

Now plot the contour:

# theme
ggthemr("flat")

# plot
gg.NB = Figure2 +
    stat_contour(aes(x=plate_x,y=plate_z,z=ypred), data=d,breaks=c(0.5),
                 geom="polygon",fill=adjustcolor("firebrick3",alpha.f=0.2))

# figure notes
annotate_figure(gg.NB,
  bottom=text_grob(
    "Note: The black rectangle represents the approximate rule book strike zone.\n Red shaded area represents the Estimated Ump Zone using each methodology.\n Inside the area, the model predicts a strike. Outside, the model predicts a ball.\nSource: Statcast Pitch Data (Toronto vs. Texas, April 2 2022).",
